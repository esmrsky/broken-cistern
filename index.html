One shape,
many wells

Broken cisterns, habit loops, the shame spiral, counterfeit needs, works vs. grace — read closely, they share one recurring shape. Not identical things; the same move wearing different clothes, and it runs through your ordinary Tuesday.

“They have committed two evils: they have forsaken the fountain of living water, and hewed out cisterns for themselves — broken cisterns that can hold no water.”
JEREMIAH 2:13 — the verse this whole page hangs on
The one sentence

A real and legitimate desire gets routed to a false source — and the falseness of the source is exactly what makes it loop.

my reading  Augustine put the same thing as disordered loves: the trouble usually isn’t wanting something evil, it’s wanting something good in the wrong order or from the wrong place. The thirst is designed and right. The well is broken. Most of the suffering here comes from blaming the desire (“I’m weak,” “more discipline”) instead of the sourcing. Augustine formulated this as ordo amoris (ordered love) in The City of God (De civitate Dei, Book XV, Ch. 22): virtue is the proper ordering of love. Misery arises when we treat a lesser good (like status, escape, or performance) as the ultimate good, which belongs to the Source alone. Blaming the thirst (e.g. "I need more willpower") rather than the source keeps the soul running on empty.

How it turns

The loop runs on almost

If the counterfeit fully satisfied, you’d stop. Because it almost works, it reinforces — and the unmet need comes back sharper. That near-miss is the engine shared by a craving, a shame spiral, and the works treadmill.

This "almost satisfied" trigger corresponds to Kent Berridge’s neurobiological dissociation between **"wanting"** (incentive salience, driven by mesolimbic dopamine projecting from the VTA to the Nucleus Accumbens) and **"liking"** (hedonic satisfaction, driven by localized opioid hotspots). The counterfeit triggers a spike of dopaminergic anticipation, but fails to activate actual satisfaction. This negative prediction error keeps the dopamine system firing, driving you to repeat the routine even when you no longer enjoy the outcome.

Guardrails

Where this lens breaks

A frame that explains everything predicts nothing. This one earns its keep only where the loop-signature is actually present — and it does real damage when forced onto things it doesn’t fit.

✕

Not every habit is a broken cistern. Brushing your teeth, a morning walk, a standing call with a friend — neutral or good loops. The frame is about counterfeit-sourced compulsion, not behavior as such.

✕

Sometimes you’re just tired, not idolatrous. Scrolling can simply be rest; a snack can simply be hunger. Over-spiritualizing ordinary limits is its own trap — scrupulosity — and it manufactures shame the gospel was meant to remove.

✕

Effort isn’t the enemy. Grace produces work — “work out your salvation, for it is God who works in you” (Phil 2:12–13); created for good works (Eph 2:10). The cistern is effort-as-source-of-worth, not effort itself. This is not a charter for passivity.

✕

Grace is not a rival to ordinary means. “Re-source it” never means “pray instead of sleeping, instead of therapy, instead of medication, instead of accountability.” Those are often how the fountain reaches you, not competitors to it.

A field test: does the thing leave you needing it again — sharper, with shame? Or does it actually satisfy and let you move on? Only the first is a broken cistern. The second is just a good gift.
The spine · old covenant / new covenant

A standard that demands, a source that supplies

my reading  In a Pauline frame, the Law works like willpower: an external standard that demands performance but supplies no power to produce it. The New Covenant doesn’t raise the bar — it moves the source inside. (Sharpest in Protestant readings; Catholic, Orthodox, and Wesleyan traditions integrate works and participation differently.)

my reading  In a Pauline frame, the Law acts as a willpower system. In psychology, **Works** mirrors **introjected regulation** (Deci & Ryan's Self-Determination Theory)—performing to stave off guilt, processed by the brain as a high-cost, extrinsic "have-to" task. **Grace** represents **integrated motivation**—operating from a pre-established, secure identity, transforming obedience into a low-cost, intrinsic "want-to" response. The New Covenant doesn't raise the bar; it moves the source inside. (Note: Modern scholarship on the New Perspective on Paul views 'works of the law' primarily as covenant identity markers, adding historical-critical depth to this classical works-vs-grace contrast.)

Old / Law / Works

the closed loop · I am the source
  • The standard sits outside you, carved in stone — it shows the target, hands you no power.
  • Order: perform → then maybe you’re worthy. Worth is the prize you never quite reach.
  • The prohibition can arouse the craving — “do not covet,” and coveting wakes up. Rom 7:8
  • The bar moves; you re-earn tomorrow. The treadmill is the broken cistern.

New / Spirit / Grace

the open system · the source is received
  • The law is written on the heart; stone swapped for flesh, the Spirit put within. Ezek 36
  • Order flips: worth given first → behavior flows from it. Indicative before imperative.
  • “No condemnation” pulls the shame fuel out of the loop. Rom 8:1
  • Change comes by beholding, not clenching — you become what you gaze at. 2 Cor 3:18

So effort isn’t abolished — it’s relocated. Under works, effort tries to generate worth. Under grace, effort expresses a worth already given. Same sweat, opposite engine.

Common denominators

Four threads under all of it

These are the family resemblances — the reasons it reads as one shape. Where the four members diverge is the guardrails section above.

i

Wrong source

Each well structurally cannot deliver what’s sought, yet stays close enough to keep you returning. Cistern, willpower-reserve, counterfeit, self-as-justifier.

ii

The cure is the disease

The attempted solution feeds the problem. Effort entrenches the habit; shame triggers the relapse; striving never arrives. A self-defeating recursion.

iii

Partial satisfaction

It works just enough to reinforce, never enough to free. The near-miss turns a mistake into a loop. Salt water for thirst.

iv

Closed vs. received

A sealed system where the self is both agent and source — versus something taken from outside. The exit isn’t a better move inside the loop; it’s a different well.

∴

The denominator under the denominators

my reading  Strip the four down and you get one line: a good desire routed to a false source, where the falseness is what keeps it spinning. That’s why grace is the structural counterpart, not just the religious item on the list — it doesn’t shame the thirst, it relocates the well.

Two evils, two moves

Stopping isn’t the same as returning

Jeremiah names two failures, not one — so the cure has two halves. Quit digging cisterns and go back to the fountain. Do only the first and the vacuum refills on its own.

Move one · necessary, not sufficient

Stop digging

Removing the counterfeit — willpower, deleting the app, white-knuckling the diet. Real, but it only empties the vessel. An empty vessel is an invitation, not a resolution.

Move two · the half that holds

Return to the source

Bring the actual need back to a well that fills it. Without this, the loop has nowhere to go but back — the “almost” still calls.

from the text  Jesus’ picture of exactly this: an unclean spirit leaves, the house is found swept and empty, so it returns with seven worse — “the last state is worse than the first.” MATT 12:43–45. Emptiness isn’t freedom; it’s a vacancy. Replace, don’t just remove.

Where I won’t round up

The honest limits

The mechanism gap is real. The theology says beholding transforms and the Spirit empowers — it doesn’t hand you a neuroscience of how received worth rewires a craving. Many who fully hold grace still relapse. Honest claim: grace changes the terms (removes shame fuel, swaps the source, reorders worth-before-behavior) more than it instantly wins the fight.

Spiritual bypass is a live risk. “Re-source to grace” can become a way to avoid the slow, practical work — sleep, therapy, repair, sometimes medication. If the language is letting you skip the means, it’s being misused.

Is the loop ever fully over here? Unsettled. Christians have argued for centuries whether Romans 7’s “wretched man” is the ongoing believer or the pre-Christian self. The practicals land differently depending on which way you read it. I can’t adjudicate that for you.

Tradition-dependence. The crisp “law = pure demand, works vs. grace” framing is most at home in Reformed/Lutheran Protestantism; other traditions would say it under-reads participation. This is a coherent reading, not the only faithful one.

One borrowed prop, flagged: “ego depletion” — willpower as a draining battery — has largely failed to replicate since ~2015. The shame loop is real as lived experience; the fuel-tank metaphor is shaky. The “habit loop” model is useful pop-psychology, not settled science.

The willpower model, updated: The "ego depletion" model (willpower as a draining metabolic battery) has failed to replicate since 2015. Modern neuroscience replaces it with **opportunity cost** (Kurzban) and **motivational shifting** (Inzlicht) models. Willpower doesn't "run out"; rather, under stress (like shame), the brain shifts priorities from high-cost, extrinsic "have-to" tasks (Works) to "want-to" tasks (Counterfeits). Grace reduces this cognitive tax by securing identity upfront. Meanwhile, the habit loop is refined by **outcome insensitivity**—once a routine is automated in the dorsolateral striatum, the cue triggers it directly regardless of the reward's value.
